<p><b>Marvel Comics</b> is the common name and primary imprint of <b>Marvel Worldwide Inc.</b>, formerly <b>Marvel Publishing, Inc.</b> and <b>Marvel Comics Group</b>, is an American publisher of <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comic_books" class="mw-redirect" title="Comic books">comic books</a> and related media. In 2009, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">The Walt Disney Company</a> acquired <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_Entertainment" title="Marvel Entertainment">Marvel Entertainment</a>, Marvel Worldwide's parent company.</p>
<p>Marvel started in 1939 as <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timely_Comics" title="Timely Comics">Timely Publications</a>, and by the early 1950s had generally become known as <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlas_Comics_(1950s)" title="Atlas Comics (1950s)">Atlas Comics</a>. Marvel's modern incarnation dates from 1961, the year that the company launched <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Fantastic_Four" class="mw-redirect" title="The Fantastic Four">The Fantastic Four</a></i> and other superhero titles created by <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stan_Lee" title="Stan Lee">Stan Lee</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jack_Kirby" title="Jack Kirby">Jack Kirby</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Ditko" title="Steve Ditko">Steve Ditko</a> and many others.</p>
<p>Marvel counts among <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Marvel_Comics_characters" title="List of Marvel Comics characters">its characters</a> such well-known superheroes as <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Captain_America" title="Captain America">Captain America</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_Man" title="Iron Man">Iron Man</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hulk_(comics)" title="Hulk (comics)">Hulk</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thor_(Marvel_Comics)" title="Thor (Marvel Comics)">Thor</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doctor_Strange" title="Doctor Strange">Doctor Strange</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spider-Man" title="Spider-Man">Spider-Man</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolverine_(character)" title="Wolverine (character)">Wolverine</a> and <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ant-Man" title="Ant-Man">Ant-Man</a>, such teams as the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avengers_(comics)" title="Avengers (comics)">Avengers</a>, the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guardians_of_the_Galaxy_(disambiguation)" class="mw-redirect" title="Guardians of the Galaxy (disambiguation)">Guardians of the Galaxy</a>, the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fantastic_Four" title="Fantastic Four">Fantastic Four</a>, the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defenders_(comics)" title="Defenders (comics)">Defenders</a>, the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-Men" title="X-Men">X-Men</a> and the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inhumans" title="Inhumans">Inhumans</a>, and antagonists such as <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doctor_Doom" title="Doctor Doom">Doctor Doom</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Skull" title="Red Skull">Red Skull</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_Goblin" title="Green Goblin">Green Goblin</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultron" title="Ultron">Ultron</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doctor_Octopus" title="Doctor Octopus">Doctor Octopus</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thanos" title="Thanos">Thanos</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magneto_(comics)" title="Magneto (comics)">Magneto</a> and <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loki_(comics)" title="Loki (comics)">Loki</a>. Most of Marvel's fictional characters operate in a single reality known as the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_Universe" title="Marvel Universe">Marvel Universe</a>, with locations that mirror real-life cities. Characters such as Spider-Man, the Fantastic Four, the Avengers, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daredevil_(Marvel_Comics_character)" title="Daredevil (Marvel Comics character)">Daredevil</a> and Doctor Strange are based in New York City,<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> whereas the X-Men have historically been based in <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Salem,_New_York" title="North Salem, New York">Salem Center, New York</a><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> and Hulk's stories often have been set in the American Southwest.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Timely_Publications">Timely Publications</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timely_Comics" title="Timely Comics">Timely Comics</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:MarvelComics1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/9f/MarvelComics1.jpg/220px-MarvelComics1.jpg" width="220" height="316" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/9/9f/MarvelComics1.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="244" data-file-height="351" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:MarvelComics1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_Mystery_Comics" title="Marvel Mystery Comics">Marvel Comics</a></i> #1 (Oct. 1939), the first comic from Marvel precursor <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timely_Comics" title="Timely Comics">Timely Comics</a>. Cover art by <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_R._Paul" title="Frank R. Paul">Frank R. Paul</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Goodman_(publisher)" title="Martin Goodman (publisher)">Martin Goodman</a> founded the company later known as Marvel Comics under the name Timely Publications in 1939.<sup id="cite_ref-marvelcomics1_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-marvelcomics1-7">[7]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MMC4_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MMC4-8">[8]</a></sup> Martin Goodman, a <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulp_magazine" title="Pulp magazine">pulp magazine</a> publisher who had started with a <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_fiction" title="Western fiction">Western</a> pulp in 1933, was expanding into the emerging—and by then already highly popular—new medium of comic books. Launching his new line from his existing company's offices at 330 West 42nd Street, New York City, he officially held the titles of <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Editing" title="Editing">editor</a>, managing editor, and <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Business_manager" title="Business manager">business manager</a>, with Abraham Goodman officially listed as publisher.<sup id="cite_ref-MMC4_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MMC4-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Timely's first publication, <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_Mystery_Comics" title="Marvel Mystery Comics">Marvel Comics</a></i> #1 (<a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cover_date" title="Cover date">cover dated</a> Oct. 1939), included the first appearance of <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Burgos" title="Carl Burgos">Carl Burgos</a>' <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Android_(robot)" title="Android (robot)">android</a> <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superhero" title="Superhero">superhero</a> the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_Torch_(android)" title="Human Torch (android)">Human Torch</a>, and the first appearances of <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bill_Everett" title="Bill Everett">Bill Everett</a>'s <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-hero" class="mw-redirect" title="Anti-hero">anti-hero</a> <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Namor" title="Namor">Namor the Sub-Mariner</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> among other features.<sup id="cite_ref-marvelcomics1_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-marvelcomics1-7">[7]</a></sup> The issue was a great success, with it and a second printing the following month selling, combined, nearly 900,000 copies.<sup id="cite_ref-fromm_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fromm-10">[10]</a></sup> While its contents came from an outside packager, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Funnies,_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Funnies, Inc.">Funnies, Inc.</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-marvelcomics1_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-marvelcomics1-7">[7]</a></sup> Timely had its own staff in place by the following year. The company's first true editor, writer-artist <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joe_Simon" title="Joe Simon">Joe Simon</a>, teamed with artist and emerging industry notable <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jack_Kirby" title="Jack Kirby">Jack Kirby</a> to create one of the first patriotically themed superheroes,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Captain_America" title="Captain America">Captain America</a>, in <i>Captain America Comics</i> #1 (March 1941). It, too, proved a hit, with sales of nearly one million.<sup id="cite_ref-fromm_10-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-fromm-10">[10]</a></sup> Goodman formed Timely Comics, Inc., beginning with comics cover-dated April 1941 or Spring 1941.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-daniels27_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-daniels27-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>While no other Timely character would achieve the success of these three characters, some notable heroes—many of which continue to appear in modern-day <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retroactive_continuity" title="Retroactive continuity">retcon</a> appearances and flashbacks—include the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whizzer_(Robert_Frank)" title="Whizzer (Robert Frank)">Whizzer</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miss_America_(Marvel_Comics)" title="Miss America (Marvel Comics)">Miss America</a>, the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Destroyer_(Keen_Marlow)" class="mw-redirect" title="Destroyer (Keen Marlow)">Destroyer</a>, the original <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vision_(Timely_Comics)" title="Vision (Timely Comics)">Vision</a>, and the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angel_(Thomas_Halloway)" title="Angel (Thomas Halloway)">Angel</a>. Timely also published one of humor cartoonist <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basil_Wolverton" title="Basil Wolverton">Basil Wolverton</a>'s best-known features, "<a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Powerhouse_Pepper" title="Powerhouse Pepper">Powerhouse Pepper</a>",<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> as well as a line of children's <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Funny_animal" title="Funny animal">funny-animal</a> comics featuring popular characters like <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Super_Rabbit" title="Super Rabbit">Super Rabbit</a> and the duo <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ziggy_Pig_and_Silly_Seal" title="Ziggy Pig and Silly Seal">Ziggy Pig and Silly Seal</a>.</p>
<p>Goodman hired his wife's cousin,<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Stanley Lieber, as a general office assistant in 1939.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> When editor Simon left the company in late 1941,<sup id="cite_ref-mylife113-14_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mylife113-14-18">[18]</a></sup> Goodman made Lieber—by then writing pseudonymously as "<a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stan_Lee" title="Stan Lee">Stan Lee</a>"—interim editor of the comics line, a position Lee kept for decades except for three years during his military service in <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. Lee wrote extensively for Timely, contributing to a number of different titles.</p>
<p>Goodman's business strategy involved having his various magazines and comic books published by a number of corporations all operating out of the same office and with the same staff.<sup id="cite_ref-daniels27_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-daniels27-13">[13]</a></sup> One of these <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shell_corporation" title="Shell corporation">shell companies</a> through which Timely Comics was published was named Marvel Comics by at least <i>Marvel Mystery Comics</i> #55 (May 1944). As well, some comics' covers, such as <i>All Surprise Comics</i> #12 (Winter 1946–47), were labeled "A Marvel Magazine" many years before Goodman would formally adopt the name in 1961.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Atlas_Comics">Atlas Comics</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlas_Comics_(1950s)" title="Atlas Comics (1950s)">Atlas Comics (1950s)</a></div>
<p>The post-war American comic market saw superheroes falling out of fashion.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Goodman's comic book line dropped them for the most part and expanded into a wider variety of genres than even Timely had published, featuring <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horror_fiction" title="Horror fiction">horror</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Westerns" class="mw-redirect" title="Westerns">Westerns</a>, humor, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Funny_animal" title="Funny animal">funny animal</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Men%27s_adventure" class="mw-redirect" title="Men's adventure">men's adventure</a>-drama, giant monster, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crime_fiction" title="Crime fiction">crime</a>, and <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_comics" title="War comics">war comics</a>, and later adding <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jungle" title="Jungle">jungle</a> books, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romance_comics_in_the_United_States_(1946%E2%80%931975)" class="mw-redirect" title="Romance comics in the United States (1946–1975)">romance</a> titles, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spy_fiction" title="Spy fiction">espionage</a>, and even <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval" class="mw-redirect" title="Medieval">medieval</a> adventure, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bible_stories" class="mw-redirect" title="Bible stories">Bible stories</a> and sports.</p>
<p>Goodman began using the globe logo of the Atlas News Company, the newsstand-distribution company he owned,<sup id="cite_ref-stjames_21-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stjames-21">[21]</a></sup> on comics <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cover-date" class="mw-redirect" title="Cover-date">cover-dated</a> November 1951 even though another company, Kable News, continued to distribute his comics through the August 1952 issues.<sup id="cite_ref-gcdatlasglobe_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-gcdatlasglobe-22">[22]</a></sup> This globe branding united a line put out by the same publisher, staff and freelancers through 59 shell companies, from Animirth Comics to Zenith Publications.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>Atlas, rather than innovate, took a proven route of following <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fashion" title="Fashion">popular trends</a> in television and movies—<a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_fiction" title="Western fiction">Westerns</a> and war dramas prevailing for a time, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drive-in_theater" title="Drive-in theater">drive-in movie</a> monsters another time—and even other comic books, particularly the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entertaining_Comics" class="mw-redirect" title="Entertaining Comics">EC</a> <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horror_(genre)" class="mw-redirect" title="Horror (genre)">horror</a> line.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup> Atlas also published a plethora of children's and teen humor titles, including <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dan_DeCarlo" title="Dan DeCarlo">Dan DeCarlo</a>'s <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homer_the_Happy_Ghost" title="Homer the Happy Ghost">Homer the Happy Ghost</a></i> (à la <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Casper_the_Friendly_Ghost" title="Casper the Friendly Ghost">Casper the Friendly Ghost</a></i>) and <i>Homer Hooper</i> (à la <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archie_Andrews_(comics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Archie Andrews (comics)">Archie Andrews</a>). Atlas unsuccessfully attempted to revive superheroes from late 1953 to mid-1954, with the Human Torch (art by <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syd_Shores" title="Syd Shores">Syd Shores</a> and <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dick_Ayers" title="Dick Ayers">Dick Ayers</a>, variously), the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Namor" title="Namor">Sub-Mariner</a> (drawn and most stories written by <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bill_Everett" title="Bill Everett">Bill Everett</a>), and <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Captain_America" title="Captain America">Captain America</a> (writer <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stan_Lee" title="Stan Lee">Stan Lee</a>, artist <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Romita_Sr." title="John Romita Sr.">John Romita Sr.</a>). Atlas did not achieve any breakout hits and, according to Stan Lee, Atlas survived chiefly because it produced work quickly, cheaply, and at a passable quality.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Comic_Code_Authority">Comic Code Authority</span></h3>
<p>During this time, the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comic_Code_Authority" class="mw-redirect" title="Comic Code Authority">Comic Code Authority</a> made its debut in September 1954, spearheaded by German-American psychiatrist Fredrick Wortham. Wortham published the book <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seduction_of_the_Innocent" title="Seduction of the Innocent">Seduction of the Innocent</a></i> in order to force people to see that comics were impacting American youth. He believed violent comics were causing children to be reckless and were turning them into delinquents. In September 1954, comic book publishers got together to set up their own self-censorship organization—the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comics_Magazine_Association_of_America" class="mw-redirect" title="Comics Magazine Association of America">Comics Magazine Association of America</a>—in order to appease audiences. The next month, the code was published, forcing comic book companies to send their comics to them in order to gain their seal of approval. The stamp on the cover showed audiences that the comics were considered wholesome, entertaining, and educational.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marvel_Comics">Marvel Comics</span></h3>
<p>The first modern comic books under the Marvel Comics brand were the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science-fiction" class="mw-redirect" title="Science-fiction">science-fiction</a> anthology <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Journey_into_Mystery" title="Journey into Mystery">Journey into Mystery</a></i> #69 and the teen-humor title <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patsy_Walker" title="Patsy Walker">Patsy Walker</a></i> #95 (both <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cover_date" title="Cover date">cover dated</a> June 1961), which each displayed an "MC" box on its cover.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Then, in the wake of <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">DC Comics</a>' success in reviving superheroes in the late 1950s and early 1960s, particularly with the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flash_(Barry_Allen)" title="Flash (Barry Allen)">Flash</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hal_Jordan" title="Hal Jordan">Green Lantern</a>, and other members of the team the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Justice_League" title="Justice League">Justice League of America</a>, Marvel followed suit.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[n 1]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1961, writer-editor <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stan_Lee" title="Stan Lee">Stan Lee</a> revolutionized <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superhero" title="Superhero">superhero</a> comics by introducing superheroes designed to appeal to more older readers than the predominantly child audiences of the medium. Modern Marvel's first superhero team, the titular stars of <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fantastic_Four" title="Fantastic Four">The Fantastic Four</a></i> #1 (Nov. 1961),<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[29]</a></sup> broke convention with other comic book archetypes of the time by squabbling, holding grudges both deep and petty, and eschewing anonymity or secret identities in favor of celebrity status. Subsequently, Marvel comics developed a reputation for focusing on characterization and adult issues to a greater extent than most superhero comics before them, a quality which the new generation of older readers appreciated.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">[30]</a></sup> This applied to <i>The Amazing Spider-Man</i> title in particular, which turned out to be Marvel's most successful book. Its young hero suffered from self-doubt and mundane problems like any other teenager, something readers could identify with.</p>
<p>Lee and freelance artist and eventual co-plotter <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jack_Kirby" title="Jack Kirby">Jack Kirby</a>'s Fantastic Four originated in a <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">Cold War</a> culture that led their creators to revise the superhero conventions of previous eras to better reflect the psychological spirit of their age.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[31]</a></sup> Eschewing such comic-book tropes as secret identities and even costumes at first, having a monster as one of the heroes, and having its characters bicker and complain in what was later called a "superheroes in the real world" approach, the series represented a change that proved to be a great success.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[32]</a></sup></p>
<p>Marvel often presented flawed superheroes, freaks, and misfits—unlike the perfect, handsome, athletic heroes found in previous traditional comic books. Some Marvel heroes looked like villains and monsters such as the Hulk and the Thing. This <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naturalism_(literature)" title="Naturalism (literature)">naturalistic</a> approach even extended into topical politics.</p>
<p>Comics historian Mike Benton also noted:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>In the world of [rival <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">DC Comics</a>'] <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superman" title="Superman">Superman</a> comic books, communism did not exist. Superman rarely crossed national borders or involved himself in political disputes.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[33]</a></sup> From 1962 to 1965, there were more communists [in Marvel Comics] than on the subscription list of <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pravda" title="Pravda">Pravda</a></i>. Communist agents attack Ant-Man in his laboratory, red henchmen jump the Fantastic Four on the moon, and <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viet_Cong" title="Viet Cong">Viet Cong</a> guerrillas take potshots at Iron Man.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[34]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>All of these elements struck a chord with the older readers, such as college-aged adults, and they successfully gained in a way not seen before. In 1965, Spider-Man and the Hulk were both featured in <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Esquire_(magazine)" title="Esquire (magazine)">Esquire</a></i> magazine's list of 28 college campus heroes, alongside <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_F._Kennedy" title="John F. Kennedy">John F. Kennedy</a> and <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bob_Dylan" title="Bob Dylan">Bob Dylan</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[35]</a></sup> In 2009 writer Geoff Boucher reflected that, "Superman and DC Comics instantly seemed like boring old <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pat_Boone" title="Pat Boone">Pat Boone</a>; Marvel felt like <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Beatles" title="The Beatles">The Beatles</a> and the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Invasion" title="British Invasion">British Invasion</a>. It was Kirby's artwork with its tension and <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychedelia" title="Psychedelia">psychedelia</a> that made it perfect for the times—or was it Lee's bravado and melodrama, which was somehow insecure and brash at the same time?"<sup id="cite_ref-laxgb_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-laxgb-37">[36]</a></sup></p>
<p>In addition to <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spider-Man" title="Spider-Man">Spider-Man</a> and the Fantastic Four, Marvel began publishing further superhero titles featuring such heroes and antiheroes as the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hulk_(comics)" title="Hulk (comics)">Hulk</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thor_(Marvel_Comics)" title="Thor (Marvel Comics)">Thor</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Pym" class="mw-redirect" title="Henry Pym">Ant-Man</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_Man" title="Iron Man">Iron Man</a>, the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-Men" title="X-Men">X-Men</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daredevil_(Marvel_Comics_character)" title="Daredevil (Marvel Comics character)">Daredevil</a>, the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inhumans" title="Inhumans">Inhumans</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Panther_(comics)" title="Black Panther (comics)">Black Panther</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doctor_Strange" title="Doctor Strange">Doctor Strange</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Captain_Marvel_(Marvel_Comics)" title="Captain Marvel (Marvel Comics)">Captain Marvel</a> and the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silver_Surfer" title="Silver Surfer">Silver Surfer</a>, and such memorable antagonists as <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doctor_Doom" title="Doctor Doom">Doctor Doom</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magneto_(comics)" title="Magneto (comics)">Magneto</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactus" title="Galactus">Galactus</a>, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loki_(comics)" title="Loki (comics)">Loki</a>, the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_Goblin" title="Green Goblin">Green Goblin</a>, and <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doctor_Octopus" title="Doctor Octopus">Doctor Octopus</a>, all existing in a shared reality known as the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_Universe" title="Marvel Universe">Marvel Universe</a>, with locations that mirror real-life cities such as New York, Los Angeles and Chicago.</p>
<p>Marvel even lampooned itself and other comics companies in a <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parody" title="Parody">parody</a> comic, <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Not_Brand_Echh" title="Not Brand Echh">Not Brand Echh</a></i> (a play on Marvel's dubbing of other companies as "Brand Echh", à la the then-common phrase "Brand X").<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[37]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cadence_Industries_ownership">Cadence Industries ownership</span></h3>
<p>In 1968, while selling 50 million comic books a year, company founder Goodman revised the constraining distribution arrangement with Independent News he had reached under duress during the Atlas years, allowing him now to release as many titles as demand warranted.<sup id="cite_ref-stjames_21-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stjames-21">[21]</a></sup> Late that year he sold Marvel Comics and his other publishing businesses to the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cadence_Industries" title="Cadence Industries">Perfect Film and Chemical Corporation</a>, which continued to group them as the subsidiary <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magazine_Management" title="Magazine Management">Magazine Management Company</a>, with Goodman remaining as publisher.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">[38]</a></sup> In 1969, Goodman finally ended his distribution deal with Independent by signing with <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curtis_Circulation" title="Curtis Circulation">Curtis Circulation Company</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-stjames_21-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-stjames-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1971, the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Health_and_Human_Services" title="United States Department of Health and Human Services">United States Department of Health, Education, and Welfare</a> approached Marvel Comics editor-in-chief <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stan_Lee" title="Stan Lee">Stan Lee</a> to do a comic book story about drug abuse. Lee agreed and wrote a three-part <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spider-Man" title="Spider-Man">Spider-Man</a> story portraying drug use as dangerous and unglamorous. However, the industry's self-censorship board, the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comics_Code_Authority" title="Comics Code Authority">Comics Code Authority</a>, refused to approve the story because of the presence of narcotics, deeming the context of the story irrelevant. Lee, with Goodman's approval, published the story regardless in <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Amazing_Spider-Man" title="The Amazing Spider-Man">The Amazing Spider-Man</a></i> #96–98 (May–July 1971), without the Comics Code seal. The market reacted well to the storyline, and the CCA subsequently revised the Code the same year.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[39]</a></sup></p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Howard_The_Duck_-8.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/d7/Howard_The_Duck_-8.jpg/220px-Howard_The_Duck_-8.jpg" width="220" height="348" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/d/d7/Howard_The_Duck_-8.jpg 1.5x" data-file-width="300" data-file-height="475" /></a>

<p>Goodman retired as publisher in 1972 and installed his son, Chip, as publisher,<sup id="cite_ref-Ronin_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ronin-41">[40]</a></sup> Shortly thereafter, Lee succeeded him as publisher and also became Marvel's president<sup id="cite_ref-Ronin_41-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ronin-41">[40]</a></sup> for a brief time.<sup id="cite_ref-autobio_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-autobio-42">[41]</a></sup> During his time as president, he appointed as editor-in-chief <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roy_Thomas" title="Roy Thomas">Roy Thomas</a>, who added "Stan Lee Presents" to the opening page of each comic book.<sup id="cite_ref-Ronin_41-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ronin-41">[40]</a></sup></p>
<p>A series of new editors-in-chief oversaw the company during another slow time for the industry. Once again, Marvel attempted to diversify, and with the updating of the Comics Code achieved moderate to strong success with titles themed to <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horror_(genre)" class="mw-redirect" title="Horror (genre)">horror</a> (<i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Tomb_of_Dracula" title="The Tomb of Dracula">The Tomb of Dracula</a></i>), martial arts, <i>(<a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Master_of_Kung_Fu_(comics)" title="Master of Kung Fu (comics)">Shang-Chi: Master of Kung Fu</a></i>), <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sword-and-sorcery" class="mw-redirect" title="Sword-and-sorcery">sword-and-sorcery</a> (<i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conan_(Marvel_Comics)" title="Conan (Marvel Comics)">Conan the Barbarian</a></i>, <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_Sonja" title="Red Sonja">Red Sonja</a></i>), satire (<i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Howard_the_Duck" title="Howard the Duck">Howard the Duck</a></i>) and science fiction (<i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2001:_A_Space_Odyssey_(comics)" title="2001: A Space Odyssey (comics)">2001: A Space Odyssey</a></i>, "<a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Killraven" title="Killraven">Killraven</a>" in <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amazing_Adventures" title="Amazing Adventures">Amazing Adventures</a></i>, <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battlestar_Galactica_(comics)" title="Battlestar Galactica (comics)">Battlestar Galactica</a></i>, <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_Trek_(comics)" title="Star Trek (comics)">Star Trek</a></i>, and, late in the decade, the long-running <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_Wars_(comics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Star Wars (comics)">Star Wars</a></i> series). Some of these were published in larger-format black and white magazines, under its <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curtis_Magazines" class="mw-redirect" title="Curtis Magazines">Curtis Magazines</a> imprint. Marvel was able to capitalize on its successful superhero comics of the previous decade by acquiring a new newsstand distributor and greatly expanding its comics line. Marvel pulled ahead of rival <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">DC Comics</a> in 1972, during a time when the price and format of the standard newsstand comic were in flux.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">[42]</a></sup> Goodman increased the price and size of Marvel's November 1971 cover-dated comics from 15 cents for 36 pages total to 25 cents for 52 pages. DC followed suit, but Marvel the following month dropped its comics to 20 cents for 36 pages, offering a lower-priced product with a higher distributor discount.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44">[43]</a></sup></p>
<p>Goodman, now disconnected from Marvel, set up a new company called <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlas/Seaboard_Comics" title="Atlas/Seaboard Comics">Seaboard Periodicals</a> in 1974, reviving Marvel's old Atlas name for a new <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlas/Seaboard_Comics" title="Atlas/Seaboard Comics">Atlas Comics</a> line, but this lasted only a year and a half.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45">[44]</a></sup> In the mid-1970s a decline of the newsstand distribution network affected Marvel. Cult hits such as <i>Howard the Duck</i> fell victim to the distribution problems, with some titles reporting low sales when in fact the first specialty comic book stores resold them at a later date.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> But by the end of the decade, Marvel's fortunes were reviving, thanks to the rise of <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_market" title="Direct market">direct market</a> distribution—selling through those same comics-specialty stores instead of newsstands.</p>
<p>Marvel held its own <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comic_book_convention" title="Comic book convention">comic book convention</a>, Marvelcon '75, in spring 1975, and promised a Marvelcon '76. At the 1975 event, Stan Lee used a <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fantastic_Four" title="Fantastic Four">Fantastic Four</a> panel discussion to announce that <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jack_Kirby" title="Jack Kirby">Jack Kirby</a>, the artist co-creator of most of Marvel's signature characters, was returning to Marvel after having left in 1970 to work for rival <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">DC Comics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-46" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-46">[45]</a></sup> In October 1976, Marvel, which already licensed reprints in different countries, including the UK, created a superhero specifically for the British market. <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Captain_Britain" title="Captain Britain">Captain Britain</a> debuted exclusively in the UK, and later appeared in American comics.<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">[46]</a></sup></p>

<p>In 1978, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jim_Shooter" title="Jim Shooter">Jim Shooter</a> became Marvel's editor-in-chief. Although a controversial personality, Shooter cured many of the procedural ills at Marvel, including repeatedly missed deadlines. During Shooter's nine-year tenure as editor-in-chief, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chris_Claremont" title="Chris Claremont">Chris Claremont</a> and <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Byrne_(comics)" title="John Byrne (comics)">John Byrne</a>'s run on the <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncanny_X-Men" title="Uncanny X-Men">Uncanny X-Men</a></i> and <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_Miller_(comics)" title="Frank Miller (comics)">Frank Miller</a>'s run on <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daredevil_(Marvel_Comics_series)" title="Daredevil (Marvel Comics series)">Daredevil</a></i> became critical and commercial successes.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">[48]</a></sup> Shooter brought Marvel into the rapidly evolving <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_market" title="Direct market">direct market</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">[49]</a></sup> institutionalized creator royalties, starting with the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epic_Comics" title="Epic Comics">Epic Comics</a> imprint for <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creator_ownership" class="mw-redirect" title="Creator ownership">creator-owned</a> material in 1982; introduced company-wide crossover story arcs with <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_Super_Hero_Contest_of_Champions" title="Marvel Super Hero Contest of Champions">Contest of Champions</a></i> and <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secret_Wars" title="Secret Wars">Secret Wars</a></i>; and in 1986 launched the ultimately unsuccessful <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Universe" title="New Universe">New Universe</a> line to commemorate the 25th anniversary of the Marvel Comics imprint. <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_Comics" title="Star Comics">Star Comics</a>, a children-oriented line differing from the regular Marvel titles, was briefly successful during this period.</p>
<p>Despite Marvel's successes in the early 1980s, it lost ground to rival DC in the latter half of the decade as many former Marvel stars defected to the competitor. DC scored critical and sales victories<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">[50]</a></sup> with titles and <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limited_series" class="mw-redirect" title="Limited series">limited series</a> such as <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Watchmen" title="Watchmen">Watchmen</a></i>, <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Batman:_The_Dark_Knight_Returns" class="mw-redirect" title="Batman: The Dark Knight Returns">Batman: The Dark Knight Returns</a></i>, <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crisis_on_Infinite_Earths" title="Crisis on Infinite Earths">Crisis on Infinite Earths</a></i>, Byrne's revamp of <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superman" title="Superman">Superman</a>, and <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alan_Moore" title="Alan Moore">Alan Moore</a>'s <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swamp_Thing" title="Swamp Thing">Swamp Thing</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marvel_Entertainment_Group_ownership">Marvel Entertainment Group ownership</span></h3>
<p>In 1986, Marvel's parent, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_Entertainment_Group" class="mw-redirect" title="Marvel Entertainment Group">Marvel Entertainment Group</a> (MEG), was sold to <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_World_Communications" class="mw-redirect" title="New World Communications">New World Entertainment</a>, which within three years sold it to <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MacAndrews_and_Forbes" class="mw-redirect" title="MacAndrews and Forbes">MacAndrews and Forbes</a>, owned by <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revlon" title="Revlon">Revlon</a> executive <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ronald_Perelman" title="Ronald Perelman">Ronald Perelman</a> in 1989. In 1991 Perelman took MEG public. Following the rapid rise of this stock, Perelman issued a series of <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Junk_bond" class="mw-redirect" title="Junk bond">junk bonds</a> that he used to acquire other entertainment companies, secured by MEG stock.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_52-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-52">[51]</a></sup></p>

<p>Marvel earned a great deal of money and recognition during the comic book boom of the early 1990s, launching the successful <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_2099" title="Marvel 2099">2099</a> line of comics set in the future (<i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spider-Man_2099" title="Spider-Man 2099">Spider-Man 2099</a></i>, etc.) and the creatively daring though commercially unsuccessful <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Razorline" title="Razorline">Razorline</a> imprint of <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superhero" title="Superhero">superhero</a> comics created by novelist and filmmaker <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clive_Barker" title="Clive Barker">Clive Barker</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[52]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[53]</a></sup> In 1990, Marvel began selling <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_Universe_Cards" title="Marvel Universe Cards">Marvel Universe Cards</a> with trading card maker <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SkyBox_International" title="SkyBox International">SkyBox International</a>. These were collectible trading cards that featured the characters and events of the Marvel Universe. The 1990s saw the rise of <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variant_cover" title="Variant cover">variant covers</a>, cover enhancements, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_Illustrated_Swimsuit" title="Marvel Illustrated Swimsuit">swimsuit issues</a>, and company-wide crossovers that affected the overall continuity of the fictional <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_Universe" title="Marvel Universe">Marvel Universe</a></p>
<p>Marvel suffered a blow in early 1992, when seven of its most prized artists — <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Todd_McFarlane" title="Todd McFarlane">Todd McFarlane</a> (known for his work on <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Parker:_Spider-Man" title="Peter Parker: Spider-Man">Spider-Man</a></i>), <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jim_Lee" title="Jim Lee">Jim Lee</a> (<i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-Men" title="X-Men">X-Men</a></i>), <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rob_Liefeld" title="Rob Liefeld">Rob Liefeld</a> (<i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-Force" title="X-Force">X-Force</a></i>), <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marc_Silvestri" title="Marc Silvestri">Marc Silvestri</a> (<i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolverine_(character)" title="Wolverine (character)">Wolverine</a></i>), <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erik_Larsen" title="Erik Larsen">Erik Larsen</a> (<i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Amazing_Spider-Man" title="The Amazing Spider-Man">The Amazing Spider-Man</a></i>), <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jim_Valentino" title="Jim Valentino">Jim Valentino</a> (<i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guardians_of_the_Galaxy_(1969_team)" title="Guardians of the Galaxy (1969 team)">Guardians of the Galaxy</a></i>), and <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whilce_Portacio" title="Whilce Portacio">Whilce Portacio</a> — left to form <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_Comics" title="Image Comics">Image Comics</a><sup id="cite_ref-TCJ_55-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TCJ-55">[54]</a></sup> in a deal brokered by <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malibu_Comics" title="Malibu Comics">Malibu Comics</a>' owner <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scott_Mitchell_Rosenberg" title="Scott Mitchell Rosenberg">Scott Mitchell Rosenberg</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-MULLIGAN_56-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MULLIGAN-56">[55]</a></sup> Three years later Rosenberg sold Malibu to Marvel on November 3, 1994,<sup id="cite_ref-MULLIGAN_56-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MULLIGAN-56">[55]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Ehrenreich_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ehrenreich-57">[56]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58">[57]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Indy.27_1994_p._7_59-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Indy.27_1994_p._7-59">[58]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Indy.27_1994_p._7_59-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Indy.27_1994_p._7-59">[58]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60">[59]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61">[60]</a></sup> who acquired the then-leading standard for computer coloring of comic books (developed by Rosenberg) in the process,<sup id="cite_ref-Wizard_World_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wizard_World-62">[61]</a></sup> but also integrating the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protectors" title="Protectors">Genesis Universe</a> (<i>Earth-1136</i>) and the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraverse" title="Ultraverse">Ultraverse</a> (<i>Earth-93060</i>) into Marvel's <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiverse_(Marvel_Comics)" title="Multiverse (Marvel Comics)">multiverse</a>.</p>

<p>In late 1994, Marvel acquired the comic book distributor <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heroes_World_Distribution" title="Heroes World Distribution">Heroes World Distribution</a> to use as its own exclusive distributor.<sup id="cite_ref-DuinCC_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DuinCC-63">[62]</a></sup> As the industry's other major publishers made exclusive distribution deals with other companies, the ripple effect resulted in the survival of only one other major distributor in North America, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diamond_Comic_Distributors_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="Diamond Comic Distributors Inc.">Diamond Comic Distributors Inc.</a><sup id="cite_ref-64" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-64">[63]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bNet_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bNet-65">[64]</a></sup> Then, by the middle of the decade, the industry had slumped, and in December 1996 MEG filed for <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chapter_11,_Title_11,_United_States_Code" title="Chapter 11, Title 11, United States Code">Chapter 11</a> bankruptcy protection.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_52-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-52">[51]</a></sup> In early 1997, when Marvel's Heroes World endeavor failed, Diamond also forged an exclusive deal with Marvel<sup id="cite_ref-66" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-66">[65]</a></sup>—giving the company its own section of its comics catalog <i>Previews</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-DuinDiamond_67-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DuinDiamond-67">[66]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1996, Marvel had some of its titles participate in "<a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heroes_Reborn_(comics)" title="Heroes Reborn (comics)">Heroes Reborn</a>", a crossover that allowed Marvel to relaunch some of its flagship characters such as the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avengers_(comics)" title="Avengers (comics)">Avengers</a> and the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fantastic_Four" title="Fantastic Four">Fantastic Four</a>, and <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outsource" class="mw-redirect" title="Outsource">outsource</a> them to the studios of two of the former Marvel artists turned Image Comics founders, Jim Lee and Rob Liefeld. The relaunched titles, which saw the characters transported to a parallel universe with a history distinct from the mainstream Marvel Universe, were a solid success amidst a generally struggling industry,<sup id="cite_ref-68" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-68">[67]</a></sup> but Marvel discontinued the experiment after a one-year run and returned the characters to the Marvel Universe proper.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Marvel_Enterprises">Marvel Enterprises</span></h3>
<p>In 1997, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toy_Biz" class="mw-redirect" title="Toy Biz">Toy Biz</a> and MEG merged to end the bankruptcy, forming a new corporation, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_Entertainment" title="Marvel Entertainment">Marvel Enterprises</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT_52-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-52">[51]</a></sup> With his business partner <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avi_Arad" title="Avi Arad">Avi Arad</a>, publisher <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bill_Jemas" title="Bill Jemas">Bill Jemas</a>, and editor-in-chief <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bob_Harras" title="Bob Harras">Bob Harras</a>, Toy Biz co-owner <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Perlmutter" title="Isaac Perlmutter">Isaac Perlmutter</a> helped stabilize the comics line.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69">[68]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1998, the company launched the imprint <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_Knights" title="Marvel Knights">Marvel Knights</a>, taking place just outside Marvel continuity with better production qualtity. The imprint was helmed by soon-to-become editor-in-chief <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joe_Quesada" title="Joe Quesada">Joe Quesada</a>, it featured tough, gritty stories showcasing such characters as the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daredevil_(Marvel_Comics_character)" title="Daredevil (Marvel Comics character)">Daredevil</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Newsarama_70-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Newsarama-70">[69]</a></sup> <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inhumans" title="Inhumans">Inhumans</a> and <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Panther_(comics)" title="Black Panther (comics)">Black Panther</a>.</p>
<p>With the new millennium, Marvel Comics emerged from bankruptcy and again began diversifying its offerings. In 2001, Marvel withdrew from the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comics_Code_Authority" title="Comics Code Authority">Comics Code Authority</a> and established its own <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_Rating_System" title="Marvel Rating System">Marvel Rating System</a> for comics. The first title from this era to not have the code was <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-Force" title="X-Force">X-Force</a></i> #119 (October 2001). Marvel also created new <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imprint_(trade_name)" title="Imprint (trade name)">imprints</a>, such as <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MAX_(comics)" class="mw-redirect" title="MAX (comics)">MAX</a> (an explicit-content line) and <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_Adventures" title="Marvel Adventures">Marvel Adventures</a> (developed for child audiences). In addition, the company created an <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiverse" title="Multiverse">alternate universe</a> imprint, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultimate_Marvel" title="Ultimate Marvel">Ultimate Marvel</a>, that allowed the company to <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reboot_(continuity)" class="mw-redirect" title="Reboot (continuity)">reboot</a> its major titles by revising and updating its characters to introduce to a new generation.</p>
<p>Some of its characters have been turned into successful film franchises, such as the <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Men_in_Black_(film)" title="Men in Black (film)">Men in Black</a></i> movie series, starting in 1997, <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blade_(film)" title="Blade (film)">Blade</a></i> movie series, starting in 1998, <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-Men_(film)" title="X-Men (film)">X-Men</a></i> movie series, starting in 2000, and the highest grossing series <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spider-Man_(2002_film)" title="Spider-Man (2002 film)">Spider-Man</a></i>, beginning in 2002.<sup id="cite_ref-71" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-71">[70]</a></sup></p>
<p>In a cross-promotion, the November 1, 2006, episode of the CBS soap opera <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Guiding_Light" class="mw-redirect" title="The Guiding Light">The Guiding Light</a></i>, titled "She's a Marvel", featured the character Harley Davidson Cooper (played by <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beth_Ehlers" title="Beth Ehlers">Beth Ehlers</a>) as a superheroine named the Guiding Light.<sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72">[71]</a></sup> The character's story continued in an eight-page backup feature, "A New Light", that appeared in several Marvel titles published November 1 and 8.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73">[72]</a></sup> Also that year, Marvel created a <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wiki" title="Wiki">wiki</a> on its Web site.<sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74">[73]</a></sup></p>
<p>In late 2007 the company launched <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_Digital_Comics_Unlimited" class="mw-redirect" title="Marvel Digital Comics Unlimited">Marvel Digital Comics Unlimited</a>, a digital archive of over 2,500 back issues available for viewing, for a monthly or annual subscription fee.<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75">[74]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2009 Marvel Comics closed its Open Submissions Policy, in which the company had accepted unsolicited samples from aspiring comic book artists, saying the time-consuming review process had produced no suitably professional work.<sup id="cite_ref-closedpolicy_76-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-closedpolicy-76">[75]</a></sup> The same year, the company commemorated its 70th anniversary, dating to its inception as <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timely_Comics" title="Timely Comics">Timely Comics</a>, by issuing the one-shot <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_Mystery_Comics" title="Marvel Mystery Comics">Marvel Mystery Comics</a> 70th Anniversary Special</i> #1 and a variety of other special issues.<sup id="cite_ref-77" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-77">[76]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-78" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-78">[77]</a></sup></p>

<p>On August 31, 2009, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">The Walt Disney Company</a> announced a deal to acquire Marvel Comics' parent corporation, Marvel Entertainment, for $4 billion<sup id="cite_ref-Disney_79-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Disney-79">[78]</a></sup> or $4.2 billion,<sup id="cite_ref-spi_80-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spi-80">[79]</a></sup> with Marvel <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shareholder" title="Shareholder">shareholders</a> to receive $30 and 0.745 Disney shares for each share of Marvel they own.<sup id="cite_ref-Disney_79-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Disney-79">[78]</a></sup> As of 2008, Marvel and its major, longtime competitor <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DC_Comics" title="DC Comics">DC Comics</a> shared over 80% of the American comic-book market.<sup id="cite_ref-81" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-81">[80]</a></sup> As of September 2010, Marvel switched its bookstores distribution company from <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diamond_Comic_Distributors" title="Diamond Comic Distributors">Diamond Book Distributors</a> to <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hachette_Distribution_Services" title="Hachette Distribution Services">Hachette Distribution Services</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-82" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-82">[81]</a></sup></p>
<p>Marvel relaunched the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CrossGen" title="CrossGen">CrossGen</a> imprint, owned by <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disney_Publishing_Worldwide" title="Disney Publishing Worldwide">Disney Publishing Worldwide</a>, in March 2011.<sup id="cite_ref-83" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-83">[82]</a></sup> Marvel and Disney Publishing began jointly publishing <i>Disney/Pixar Presents</i> magazine that May.<sup id="cite_ref-84" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-84">[83]</a></sup></p>
<p>Marvel discontinued its <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_Adventures" title="Marvel Adventures">Marvel Adventures</a> imprint in March 2012,<sup id="cite_ref-85" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-85">[84]</a></sup> and replaced them with a line of two titles connected to the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_Universe_(TV)" class="mw-redirect" title="Marvel Universe (TV)">Marvel Universe TV block</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-86" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-86">[85]</a></sup> Also in March, Marvel announced its Marvel ReEvolution initiative that included Infinite Comics,<sup id="cite_ref-lat_87-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lat-87">[86]</a></sup> a line of <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_comics" class="mw-redirect" title="Digital comics">digital comics</a>, Marvel AR, an <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Application_software" title="Application software">application software</a> that provides an <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augmented_reality" title="Augmented reality">augmented reality</a> experience to readers and <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_NOW!" title="Marvel NOW!">Marvel NOW!</a>, a relaunch of most of the company's major titles with different creative teams.<sup id="cite_ref-ReEvolution1_88-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ReEvolution1-88">[87]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NOW.21_89-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NOW.21-89">[88]</a></sup> Marvel NOW! also saw the debut of new flagship titles including <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncanny_Avengers" title="Uncanny Avengers">Uncanny Avengers</a></i> and <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/All-New_X-Men" title="All-New X-Men">All-New X-Men</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-launch_90-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-launch-90">[89]</a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2013, Marvel and other Disney conglomerate components began announcing joint projects. With ABC, a <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Once_Upon_a_Time_(TV_series)#Graphic_novel" title="Once Upon a Time (TV series)"><i>Once Upon a Time</i> graphic novel</a> was announced for publication in September.<sup id="cite_ref-91" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-91">[90]</a></sup> With Disney, Marvel announced in October 2013 that in January 2014 it would release its first title under their joint "Disney Kingdoms" imprint "Seekers of the Weird", a five-issue miniseries.<sup id="cite_ref-spi_80-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-spi-80">[79]</a></sup> On January 3, 2014, fellow Disney subsidiary <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lucasfilm" title="Lucasfilm">Lucasfilm Limited, LLC</a> announced that as of 2015, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_Wars_(comics)" class="mw-redirect" title="Star Wars (comics)"><i>Star Wars</i> comics</a> would once again be published by Marvel.<sup id="cite_ref-92" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-92">[91]</a></sup></p>
<p>Following the events of the company-wide crossover <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secret_Wars_(2015_comic_book)" title="Secret Wars (2015 comic book)">Secret Wars</a></i> in 2015, a relaunched Marvel universe began in September 2015, called the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/All-New,_All-Different_Marvel" title="All-New, All-Different Marvel">All-New, All-Different Marvel</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-93" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-93">[92]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Editors-in-chief">Editors-in-chief</span></h3>
<p>Marvel's chief editor originally held the title of "editor". This head editor's title later became "editor-in-chief". <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joe_Simon" title="Joe Simon">Joe Simon</a> was the company's first true chief-editor, with publisher <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Goodman_(publisher)" title="Martin Goodman (publisher)">Martin Goodman</a>, who had served as titular editor only and outsourced editorial operations.</p>
<p>In 1994 Marvel briefly abolished the position of editor-in-chief, replacing <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tom_DeFalco" title="Tom DeFalco">Tom DeFalco</a> with five group editors-in-chief. As Carl Potts described the 1990s editorial arrangement:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>In the early '90s, Marvel had so many titles that there were three Executive Editors, each overseeing approximately 1/3 of the line. <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bob_Budiansky" title="Bob Budiansky">Bob Budiansky</a> was the third Executive Editor [following the previously appointed <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_Gruenwald" title="Mark Gruenwald">Mark Gruenwald</a> and Potts]. We all answered to Editor-in-Chief Tom DeFalco and Publisher <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Hobson" class="mw-redirect" title="Michael Hobson">Mike Hobson</a>. All three Executive Editors decided not to add our names to the already crowded credits on the Marvel titles. Therefore it wasn't easy for readers to tell which titles were produced by which Executive Editor … In late '94, Marvel reorganized into a number of different publishing divisions, each with its own Editor-in-Chief.<sup id="cite_ref-pop_97-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pop-97">[96]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Marvel reinstated the overall editor-in-chief position in 1995 with <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bob_Harras" title="Bob Harras">Bob Harras</a>.</p>

<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Role-playing">Role-playing</span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Marvel_RPG_supplements" title="List of Marvel RPG supplements">List of Marvel RPG supplements</a></div>
<p><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TSR,_Inc." class="mw-redirect" title="TSR, Inc.">TSR</a> published the pen-and-paper <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Role-playing_game" title="Role-playing game">role-playing game</a> <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_Super_Heroes_(role-playing_game)" title="Marvel Super Heroes (role-playing game)">Marvel Super Heroes</a> in 1984. TSR then released in 1998 the <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_Super_Heroes_Adventure_Game" title="Marvel Super Heroes Adventure Game">Marvel Super Heroes Adventure Game</a></i> which used a different system, the card-based SAGA system, than their first game. In 2003 Marvel Publishing published its own role-playing game, the <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_Universe_Roleplaying_Game" title="Marvel Universe Roleplaying Game">Marvel Universe Roleplaying Game</a></i>, that used a diceless stone pool system.<sup id="cite_ref-108" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-108">[107]</a></sup> In August 2011 <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Margaret_Weis_Productions" title="Margaret Weis Productions">Margaret Weis Productions</a> announced it was developing a <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tabletop_role-playing_game" title="Tabletop role-playing game">tabletop role-playing game</a> based on the Marvel universe, set for release in February 2012 using its house Cortex Plus RPG system.<sup id="cite_ref-109" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-109">[108]</a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Video_games">Video games</span></h4>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_video_games_based_on_Marvel_Comics" title="List of video games based on Marvel Comics">List of video games based on Marvel Comics</a></div>
<p>Video games based on Marvel characters go back to 1984 and the Atari game, <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spider-Man_(Atari_2600_video_game)" title="Spider-Man (Atari 2600 video game)">Spider-Man</a></i>. Since then several dozen video games have been released and all have been produced by outside licensees. In 2014, <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disney_Infinity_2.0:_Marvel_Super_Heroes" class="mw-redirect" title="Disney Infinity 2.0: Marvel Super Heroes">Disney Infinity 2.0: Marvel Super Heroes</a></i> was released that brought Marvel characters to the existing <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disney" class="mw-redirect" title="Disney">Disney</a> sandbox video game.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Films">Films</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_films_based_on_Marvel_Comics" title="List of films based on Marvel Comics">List of films based on Marvel Comics</a></div>
<p>As of the start of September 2015, films based on Marvel's properties represent the highest-grossing U.S. franchise, having grossed over $7.7 billion <sup id="cite_ref-110" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-110">[109]</a></sup> as part of a worldwide gross of over $18 billion.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Prose_novels">Prose novels</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main articles: <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_Books" title="Marvel Books">Marvel Books</a> and <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_Press" title="Marvel Press">Marvel Press</a></div>
<p>Marvel first licensed two prose novels to <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bantam_Books" title="Bantam Books">Bantam Books</a>, who printed <i>The Avengers Battle the Earth Wrecker</i> by <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otto_Binder" title="Otto Binder">Otto Binder</a> (1967) and <i>Captain America: The Great Gold Steal</i> by <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ted_White_(author)" title="Ted White (author)">Ted White</a> (1968). Various publishers took up the licenses from 1978 to 2002. Also, with the various licensed films being released beginning in 1997, various publishers put out movie <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Novelizations" class="mw-redirect" title="Novelizations">novelizations</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-mcpug_111-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcpug-111">[110]</a></sup> In 2003, following publication of the prose <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Young_adult_novel" class="mw-redirect" title="Young adult novel">young adult novel</a> <i>Mary Jane</i>, starring <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mary_Jane_Watson" title="Mary Jane Watson">Mary Jane Watson</a> from the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spider-Man" title="Spider-Man">Spider-Man</a> mythos, Marvel announced the formation of the publishing <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imprint_(trade_name)" title="Imprint (trade name)">imprint</a> <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_Press" title="Marvel Press">Marvel Press</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-cbr1_112-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbr1-112">[111]</a></sup> However, Marvel moved back to licensing with Pocket Books from 2005 to 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-mcpug_111-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mcpug-111">[110]</a></sup> With few books issued under the imprint, Marvel and <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disney_Publishing_Worldwide" title="Disney Publishing Worldwide">Disney Books Group</a> relaunched Marvel Press in 2011 with the Marvel Origin Storybooks line.<sup id="cite_ref-cbr2_113-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cbr2-113">[112]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television_programs">Television programs</span></h3>
<div role="note" class="hatnote">Main article: <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_television_series_based_on_Marvel_Comics" title="List of television series based on Marvel Comics">List of television series based on Marvel Comics</a></div>
<p>Many television series, both live-action and animated, have based their productions on Marvel Comics characters. These include multiple series for popular characters such as Spider-Man, Iron Man and the X-Men. Additionally, a handful of television movies, usually also pilots, based on Marvel Comics characters have been made.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theme_parks">Theme parks</span></h3>
<p>Marvel has licensed<sup id="cite_ref-Marvel-Universal_Contract_114-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marvel-Universal_Contract-114">[113]</a></sup> its characters for theme-parks and attractions, including at the <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_Orlando_Resort" class="mw-redirect" title="Universal Orlando Resort">Universal Orlando Resort</a>'s <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islands_of_Adventure" title="Islands of Adventure">Islands of Adventure</a>, in <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orlando,_Florida" title="Orlando, Florida">Orlando, Florida</a>, which includes rides based on their iconic characters and costumed performers.<sup id="cite_ref-115" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-115">[114]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walt_Disney_Parks_and_Resorts" title="Walt Disney Parks and Resorts">Walt Disney Parks and Resorts</a> plans on creating original Marvel attractions at their theme parks,<sup id="cite_ref-116" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-116">[115]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-117" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-117">[116]</a></sup> with <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Kong_Disneyland" title="Hong Kong Disneyland">Hong Kong Disneyland</a> becoming the first Disney theme park to feature a <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_Man_Experience" title="Iron Man Experience">Marvel attraction</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-118" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-118">[117]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-119" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-119">[118]</a></sup> Due to the licensing agreement with Universal Studios, signed prior to Disney's purchase of Marvel, Walt Disney World and Tokyo Disney are barred from having Marvel characters in their parks.<sup id="cite_ref-120" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-120">[119]</a></sup> However, this only includes characters Universal is currently using, other characters in their "families" (X-Men, Avengers, Fantastic Four, etc.), and the villains associated with said characters.<sup id="cite_ref-Marvel-Universal_Contract_114-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Marvel-Universal_Contract-114">[113]</a></sup> This clause has allowed Walt Disney World to have meet and greets, merchandise, attractions and more with other Marvel characters not associated with the characters at Islands of Adventures, such as <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star-Lord" title="Star-Lord">Star-Lord</a> and <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamora" title="Gamora">Gamora</a> from <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guardians_of_the_Galaxy_(film)" title="Guardians of the Galaxy (film)">Guardians of the Galaxy</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-121" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-121">[120]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-122" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-122">[121]</a></sup> as well as <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baymax" title="Baymax">Baymax</a> and <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hiro_Takachiho" title="Hiro Takachiho">Hiro</a> from <i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Hero_6_(film)" title="Big Hero 6 (film)">Big Hero 6</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-123" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-123">[122]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span></h2>
<div class="reflist" style="list-style-type: decimal;">
<ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-29"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-29">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Apocryphal legend has it that in 1961, either <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jack_Liebowitz" title="Jack Liebowitz">Jack Liebowitz</a> or <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irwin_Donenfeld" title="Irwin Donenfeld">Irwin Donenfeld</a> of DC Comics (then known as <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Periodical_Publications" class="mw-redirect" title="National Periodical Publications">National Periodical Publications</a>) bragged about DC's success with the Justice League (which had debuted in <i>The Brave and the Bold</i> #28 [February 1960] before going on to its own title) to <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Publisher" class="mw-redirect" title="Publisher">publisher</a> <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Goodman_(publisher)" title="Martin Goodman (publisher)">Martin Goodman</a> (whose holdings included the nascent Marvel Comics) during a game of golf.<br />
<br />
However, film producer and comics historian <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Uslan" title="Michael Uslan">Michael Uslan</a> partly debunked the story in a letter published in <i>Alter Ego</i> #43 (December 2004), pp. 43–44</span>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p><span class="reference-text">Irwin said he never played golf with Goodman, so the story is untrue. I heard this story more than a couple of times while sitting in the lunchroom at DC's 909 Third Avenue and 75 Rockefeller Plaza office as <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sol_Harrison" title="Sol Harrison">Sol Harrison</a> and [production chief] <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jack_Adler" title="Jack Adler">Jack Adler</a> were schmoozing with some of us … who worked for DC during our college summers.... [T]he way I heard the story from Sol was that Goodman was playing with one of the heads of Independent News, not DC Comics (though DC <i>owned</i> Independent News). … As the distributor of DC Comics, this man certainly knew all the sales figures and was in the best position to tell this tidbit to Goodman. … Of course, Goodman would want to be playing golf with this fellow and be in his good graces. … Sol worked closely with Independent News' top management over the decades and would have gotten this story straight from the horse's mouth.</span></p>
</blockquote>
<p><span class="reference-text">Goodman, a publishing trend-follower aware of the JLA's strong sales, confirmably directed his comics editor, <a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stan_Lee" title="Stan Lee">Stan Lee</a>, to create a comic-book series about a team of superheroes. According to Lee in <i>Origins of Marvel Comics</i> (<a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marvel_Fireside_Books" title="Marvel Fireside Books">Simon and Schuster/Fireside Books</a>, 1974), p. 16: "Martin mentioned that he had noticed one of the titles published by National Comics seemed to be selling better than most. It was a book called <i>The</i>  [<i><a href="//en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">sic</a></i>] <i>Justice League of America</i> and it was composed of a team of superheroes. … ' If the Justice League is selling ', spoke he, 'why don't we put out a comic book that features a team of superheroes?'"</span></p>
